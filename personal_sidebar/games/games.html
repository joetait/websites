Go

Go is a two player board game, and according to Wikipedia is one of the oldest board games in existence. The rules are very simple, but it is one of the most complex games in existence, as can be seen from another Wikipedia link.

The rules are listed very concisely here, and slightly less concisely, but with useful pictures, here. In my opinion the best place to play online is KGS, though there are servers where you can make a move a day or less. The classic resource for all things go related is Sensei's library. Finally, there is a thriving online go community on Reddit, which has resources page with links to everything else you could possibly want.

Learning Go

I often find it easier to learn if I feel like I am explaining what I am doing to other people. I also think that often when I read or watch videos about go that many things are assumed that I am not aware of. As a lowly 8 kyu (that is my current KGS rank, it is probably an overestimate) I intend to try and keep a biweekly (this will/should probably go down to weekly) blog style thing. In this I intend to go over joseki and tesuji that I think are cool, interesting or vital, and interesting fuseki or pro games that I am reviewing.

At some point I will make an easier referencing system, and possibly add somewhere for people to comment. In the meantime, do email me if you have any questions or comments.

Chinese 1 (low)

The Chinese fuseki (opening) is one of the most popular openings with all levels of players. It's name derives from the fact that Chinese players were the first to play the opening regularly.

The general idea is that black builds up a huge moyo (framework) which invites white to invade. When she does, black attacks, and builds territory rapidly as he does. Anyway, below I go through some of the very basic ideas, approximately following the first chapter of The Chinese opening by Kato Masao.

Monkey Jump

I look at the monkey jump, which is a significant end game move. It is very sensitive to the precise position of the surrounding stones, but the idea is always the same - a three space extension along the bottom line which reduces the opponents territory by about 6 to 10. I have given a number of positions in which it can occur, and though this is far from exhaustive, it should give the main idea. Enjoy!

High 4-4 approach

Today I shall be considering basic responses to the high 4-4 approach; i.e. if you play on the 4-4 and someone approaches on the fourth line. There will not be much detail, but I think that the idea of why each response is good or bad is clear, and from there, though the precise moves may not the same, the spirit is quite easy to follow.
